## Company Overview

### Business Description

Coherent Corp. is a global leader in photonics — the science and technology of generating, controlling, and detecting photons. The company designs, manufactures, and markets lasers, optical components, laser-based systems, and compound semiconductor materials for applications spanning AI data centers, telecommunications, industrial manufacturing, scientific research, and electric vehicles.

The modern Coherent was created on **July 1, 2022**, when II-VI Incorporated completed its $6.8 billion acquisition of the legacy Coherent Inc. (the historical COHR). The combined entity retained the Coherent name and ticker while dramatically expanding its scale, technology portfolio, and end-market exposure. CEO Jim Anderson, who joined in March 2023, has been executing a focused strategic realignment toward the company's highest-growth, highest-margin opportunities.[^3]

### Three-Segment Structure

Coherent operates across three reportable segments:

**1. Networking (~62% of FY2025 revenue, ~$3,600M)**
The Networking segment manufactures high-speed optical transceivers, laser components, optical amplifiers, and indium phosphide (InP)-based photonic integrated circuits. This is the crown jewel — the segment generating 800G and emerging 1.6T QSFP-DD/OSFP transceivers that are the lifeblood of AI training and inference clusters. Q4 FY2025 Networking revenue reached $945M, and the segment grew >40% YoY through Q3 FY2026.[^2][^4]

**2. Laser Sources (~23% of FY2025 revenue, ~$1,330M)**
The Laser Sources segment produces fiber lasers, CO₂ lasers, ultrafast lasers, and laser measurement tools for industrial cutting/welding, display manufacturing, semiconductor lithography, and medical applications. This segment is in contraction near-term following the announced divestiture of the Aerospace & Defense (A&D) business, which represented ~$200M+ of Laser revenue.[^5][^1]

**3. Materials (~15% of FY2025 revenue, ~$880M)**
The Materials segment manufactures compound semiconductor substrates, silicon carbide (SiC) wafers, gallium arsenide (GaAs) substrates, and indium phosphide wafers used in power electronics and optical communications. The SiC business is strategically ring-fenced through a $1 billion joint investment from Japan's DENSO Corporation and Mitsubishi Electric, each acquiring a 12.5% stake in Coherent's SiC subsidiary.[^6][^7]

### Management Team

**Jim Anderson, CEO** (joined March 2023): Former CEO of Lattice Semiconductor, where he orchestrated a similarly successful turnaround — stock +400% during his tenure. Anderson brought operational discipline, portfolio rationalization (Lattice exited low-margin businesses), and customer focus to COHR. His track record at COHR speaks for itself: 23% revenue growth in FY2025 with 358bps gross margin expansion.[^1]

**Sherri Luther, CFO** (current): Led financial discipline through the deleveraging program. $437M in debt repaid in FY2025. Provides credible capital allocation guidance.[^1]

## Industry & Competitive Landscape

### AI Optical Networking TAM

The global AI-driven optical transceiver market is experiencing a structural step-change. In 2024, hyperscalers (Microsoft, Amazon AWS, Google, Meta) collectively announced over $320 billion in data center capital expenditure for 2025-2026, with optical interconnects representing ~5-8% of total build-out costs. The 800G transceiver market was valued at $2.2 billion in 2023, with AI-related optical transceiver market reaching $1.4 billion, expected to grow at 14% CAGR to $2.39 billion by 2027.[^8]

However, these TAM figures understate COHR's opportunity. The transition from 400G → 800G → 1.6T represents a 4x-8x increase in unit revenue per transceiver, not just volume growth. COHR captured ~10% of the 800G market ($220M) in 2023, and management commentary in Q3 FY2026 confirms "record backlog" and "unprecedented booking levels".[^9][^8]

### COHR's Competitive Moat: InP Vertical Integration

Coherent's primary moat is its **vertical integration across the InP photonics stack**. Unlike competitors who rely on foundries, COHR designs, epitaxially grows, fabricates, and packages its own InP wafers — a process it has refined over 30+ years. The ramp of the world's first commercial 6-inch InP production line (announced Q3 FY2026) is a multi-year lead vs. competitors who remain on 4-inch.[^2][^9]

The competitive landscape includes:
- **Lumentum (LITE)**: Closest optical comp, primarily telecom-focused; AI transceiver ramp lagging COHR by 12-18 months; no vertical InP manufacturing
- **Marvell/Inphi**: Fabless DSP chip company; dependent on foundries for InP; strong in co-packaged optics (CPO) but complementary to COHR
- **II-VI (IIVI)**: Now merged into COHR; eliminated as standalone competitor
- **NTT Electronics / Acacia (Cisco)**: Telecom-focused; limited AI hyperscaler design wins vs. COHR's established relationships with Microsoft Azure, AWS, and Google

## Financial Analysis

### Revenue Trajectory: From Cyclical Trough to AI-Driven Secular Growth

Coherent's revenue history tells a clear story in three acts:

**Act I (FY2021-FY2022): Legacy Scale** — Pre-merger legacy Coherent generated $3.1-3.3B in revenue with 39-40% gross margins. This was a high-quality, niche industrial laser business.

**Act II (FY2023-FY2024): Post-Merger Integration Pain** — The combined II-VI entity recorded $5.2B in revenue in FY2023 but only because the merger closed July 2022 (thus including II-VI for the full year vs. legacy Coherent). Organic performance was poor: GAAP losses of $(2.93)/share and $(1.84)/share in FY2023-24. Gross margins compressed to 31-38% as integration costs, price concessions in weak end-markets (consumer electronics, telecom), and II-VI's lower-margin legacy businesses weighed heavily.

**Act III (FY2025-present): AI-Driven Re-Rating** — FY2025 revenue reached a record $5.81 billion, +23% YoY. The shift is structural: Networking grew ~48% vs. prior year as 800G AI transceiver demand exploded. By Q3 FY2026, COHR reported quarterly revenue of $1.81B (+21% YoY, +27% pro-forma), with Q4 FY2026 guidance of $1.91B-$2.05B, implying FY2026 full-year revenue of ~$7.06B.[^10][^1]

### Margin Recovery in Progress

The non-GAAP gross margin trajectory is the most important financial metric to track. After bottoming at ~31.0% in FY2024 (GAAP basis), margins have recovered sharply:

- FY2024 GAAP Gross Margin: ~31.0%
- FY2025 GAAP Gross Margin: 35.2% (+424bps YoY)[^1]
- FY2025 Non-GAAP Gross Margin: 37.9% (+358bps YoY)
- Q3 FY2026 Non-GAAP Gross Margin: 39.6% (+105bps YoY)[^10]

The drivers are clear: (1) mix shift toward higher-margin AI transceivers; (2) InP 6-inch manufacturing scale benefits; (3) closure of the UK fab and A&D divestiture removing lower-margin revenue.

### Cash Flow & Balance Sheet

Free cash flow has been modest due to the investment cycle, with FY2025 FCF of approximately $193 million. However, the delta between reported FCF and underlying cash generation potential is large — COHR invested $441M in CapEx in FY2025 while generating $843M Adj. EBITDA. As the InP capacity ramp matures and CapEx normalizes from ~8% to ~5-6% of revenue by FY2028, FCF should inflect sharply to $1.5-2.0B+.

Net debt stood at $2.98 billion at FY2025, with Net Debt/EBITDA at 3.3x. The $1B DENSO/Mitsubishi SiC investment (received December 2023) was a critical balance sheet stabilization event that eliminated the need for dilutive equity issuance to fund SiC capex. COHR has repaid $437M of debt in FY2025 alone.[^7][^1]

### Historical Return Metrics

ROIC has been under pressure due to the massive goodwill and intangible asset base from the II-VI merger ($5.6B goodwill at acquisition). FY2025 ROIC was approximately 0.7% — essentially zero — but improving rapidly as EBIT scales. The long-term ROIC target (10-12%) requires sustained gross margin expansion and operating leverage, both of which are tracking favorably.

## Investment Thesis Pillars

### Pillar 1: AI Optics — Structural Not Cyclical

The market has periodically treated COHR as a cyclical industrial company exposed to telecom and consumer electronics cycles. This framing is obsolete. By FY2026E, Networking (Datacenter + AI optics) will represent >73% of total revenue, and the growth is structurally driven by the multi-decade AI infrastructure buildout. Microsoft, Google, Amazon, and Meta have each committed to spending $50-100B+ annually on AI infrastructure through at least 2027. Every A100/H100/B200 GPU cluster requires optical interconnects, and COHR's 800G and 1.6T InP transceivers are the current-generation standard.[^13][^2]

### Pillar 2: CEO Anderson's Turnaround

Jim Anderson joined an organization burdened by integration complexity, excess costs, and strategic drift. His playbook — drawn directly from the Lattice Semiconductor transformation — is working:
- Gross margins expanded from ~31% (FY2024) to 37.9% (FY2025) to 39.6% (Q3FY2026)[^10][^1]
- A&D business divestiture refocuses company on photonics core
- Non-GAAP EPS improved from $1.21 (FY2024) to $3.53 (FY2025), a +192% improvement
- Consecutive earnings beats: Q1FY25, Q2FY25, Q3FY25, Q4FY25, Q1FY26, Q2FY26, Q3FY26[^2]

### Pillar 3: Deleveraging Re-Rating

The most underappreciated aspect of the COHR bull case is the mechanical re-rating from deleveraging. At ~3.3x Net Debt/EBITDA, COHR carries a significant credit risk discount in its equity valuation. As debt falls to <2x (FY2027E) and then <1x (FY2028E), multiple expansion should layer on top of fundamental EPS growth. We estimate each 0.5x reduction in leverage is worth ~$20-30/share in equity re-rating. The A&D business sale (~$350-450M estimated proceeds) will accelerate this timeline.

### Pillar 4: SiC Strategic Optionality

The Silicon Carbide business — largely funded and de-risked by the $1B DENSO/Mitsubishi investment — represents a call option on the EV market that investors are getting nearly for free. With DENSO and Mitsubishi Electric already at 25% ownership of SiC LLC, the path to a partial IPO or full strategic sale of SiC is established. SiC's standalone revenue of ~$880M (FY2025) at industry-comparable EV/Revenue multiples (3-4x) implies $2.6-3.5B of value that is only partially captured in COHR's current EV.[^6][^12]

## Risks

### Risk 1: Hyperscaler CapEx Reduction (HIGH SEVERITY / MEDIUM PROBABILITY)
If Microsoft, Google, Amazon, or Meta reduce or pause AI data center investments, COHR's Networking segment revenue could fall 25-40% from peak. Our bear case models a $1.3B revenue shortfall vs. base in FY2026-27. **Mitigant**: Backlog described as "record" through Q3 FY2026; 6-12 month forward visibility from purchase orders; share of wallet growing within hyperscaler capex.[^2]

### Risk 2: China Export Control Escalation (HIGH SEVERITY / LOW-MEDIUM PROBABILITY)
Advanced optical components used in AI data centers could be targeted by BIS export restrictions, eliminating ~12-15% of COHR revenue from Chinese cloud providers (Alibaba, Tencent, Baidu). **Mitigant**: US/EU hyperscalers are the primary growth driver; product redesign programs underway; lower-spec products likely to retain exemptions.

### Risk 3: 1.6T Ramp Execution Risk (MEDIUM SEVERITY / MEDIUM PROBABILITY)
The Q3 FY2026 earnings call flagged that management pushed back the long-term capacity ramp timeline by at least one quarter due to production bottlenecks. Any further delays could cause guidance misses and multiple compression. **Mitigant**: Bottleneck described as "resolved" in Q3; 6-inch InP line operational; multiple production sites provide redundancy.[^2]

### Risk 4: Goodwill Impairment (MEDIUM SEVERITY / LOW PROBABILITY)
COHR carried ~$5.6B of goodwill post-merger. If Materials (SiC) or Laser segments underperform for an extended period, a non-cash impairment charge is possible. **Mitigant**: Annual impairment testing; A&D divestiture already removes one weak segment; SiC LLC is largely funded independently.

### Risk 5: Competitive Displacement in 1.6T (MEDIUM / MEDIUM)
Co-packaged optics (CPO) technology advocated by Marvell could eventually reduce pluggable transceiver demand. CPO is 3-5 years from mass deployment and actually requires InP lasers made by COHR. **Mitigant**: COHR is a component supplier to both pluggable and CPO; existing hyperscaler design wins have 24-36 month qualification cycles creating switching costs.
