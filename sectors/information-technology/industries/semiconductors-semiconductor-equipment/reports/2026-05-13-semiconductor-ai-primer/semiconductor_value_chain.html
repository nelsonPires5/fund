Layer 1

EDA Software & Semiconductor IP

Why it Exists

Modern chips contain 100B+ transistors. No human can design these without EDA tools (logic synthesis, verification, layout). IP blocks (ARM cores, PCIe controllers) let designers reuse proven sub-circuits rather than rebuilding from scratch every time.

Why it's Critical

The entire design output of every fabless company — worth hundreds of billions — flows through just three EDA vendors. A design tape-out at 3nm costs $50–100M. Without correct EDA sign-off, the chip fails in silicon. There is no workaround.

Layer 2

Chip Design — Fabless & IDM

Why it Exists

Fabless companies own the intellectual value of chip architecture — the what, not the how. By outsourcing manufacturing to TSMC/Samsung, they avoid $20B+ fab investments and can iterate designs rapidly. IDMs (Intel, Samsung Logic) own both design and manufacturing.

Bottleneck / Risk

Fabless companies are entirely dependent on TSMC for leading-edge nodes. Any TSMC disruption stops product launches. NVIDIA's H100/B200 are sole-sourced from TSMC CoWoS, creating a single point of failure for the entire AI compute buildout.

Layer 3

Wafer Fabrication Equipment (WFE)

Why it Exists

A semiconductor fab cannot build a chip from silicon without WFE. Lithography machines (ASML) pattern nanometer-scale features. Deposition tools (AMAT) lay down thin films atom by atom. Etch tools carve structures. A leading-edge fab requires ~1,000 unique WFE units.

Bottleneck

ASML is the only company on earth that can make EUV lithography machines. Lead times are 12–18 months. Each machine costs $200M+. ASML's High-NA EUV (for 2nm/1.4nm nodes) is years ahead of any would-be competitor. Zero substitutes.

Layer 4

Wafer Foundry / "Foundry 2.0"

Why it Exists

Fabs cost $20–30B to build and 3–5 years to qualify. No chip designer can afford this. TSMC's model — manufacture chips for anyone, never compete in design — gave fabless companies the confidence to outsource. This separation of design and manufacturing is the structural foundation of the modern semiconductor industry.

Bottleneck

TSMC holds ~62% of global pure-play foundry revenue and 90%+ of all advanced-node (sub-5nm) capacity. There is no viable alternative for NVIDIA's H-series or Apple's A-series. Samsung Foundry is years behind on yield. Intel Foundry is unproven commercially.

Layer 5

Memory — DRAM, NAND & HBM

Why it Exists

Logic chips process data; memory chips store it. DRAM feeds data to CPUs/GPUs at high speed. NAND stores it persistently. High-Bandwidth Memory (HBM) is a critical AI-specific innovation — DRAM dies stacked 3D and integrated directly alongside GPU dies via silicon interposer, giving 8–12× the bandwidth of conventional DRAM.

The HBM Bottleneck

HBM is the #1 AI supply chain bottleneck in 2025–2027. All three HBM suppliers (SK Hynix, Samsung, Micron) are sold out through 2026. NVIDIA's B300 carries 288GB HBM3E — more than double the H200. HBM now accounts for ~2/3 of AI chip component cost. Customers are directly funding supplier capex to secure allocations.

Layer 6

Advanced Packaging & OSAT

Why it Exists

After a wafer is fabricated, individual dies must be cut, tested, and packaged to connect to a circuit board. Traditional packaging is commodity. Advanced packaging (CoWoS, InFO, HBM stacking) is now strategic — it enables AI chips to integrate multiple dies with extreme bandwidth densities impossible in a single silicon die.

Bottleneck (Solved mid-2025)

CoWoS was the primary AI supply chain bottleneck in late 2024–early 2025. The top four AI chip designers consumed nearly 100% of TSMC's CoWoS output. TSMC doubled CoWoS capacity through 2025; by mid-2025 the bottleneck shifted to HBM. AI packaging demand requires 40–50K CoWoS wafers/month by 2026.

Layer 7

Systems, AI Infrastructure & End Markets

Why it Exists

This layer converts chips into deployed computing — data center servers, networking gear, edge AI devices, smartphones, automotive. Hyperscalers (AWS, Azure, Google Cloud, Meta) have become the dominant AI infrastructure buyers, representing 60%+ of NVIDIA data center revenue.

Demand Driver

AI model training and inference is the defining demand pull of this cycle. Hyperscalers announced $300B+ in 2025 capex. ASIC momentum (Google TPU, Amazon Trainium, Meta MTIA) is creating custom chip demand via Broadcom and Marvell. This is the layer that sets the pace for all layers above.

Critical Bottlenecks & Margin Expansion Leaders

Current Bottlenecks (2025–2027)
🔴 HBM Memory — CRITICAL (2025–2027)
All three suppliers (SK Hynix, Samsung, Micron) are sold out. HBM = 2/3 of AI chip component cost. NVIDIA's B300 carries 288GB HBM3E. Customers financing supplier capex directly. No relief before 2027.
🟡 Advanced Logic (3nm/2nm) — Rising
NVIDIA's Rubin generation moves to 3nm N3P. AI chip designers will consume a growing share of advanced node capacity. TSMC Arizona capacity insufficient to meet demand alone. Risk of tighter allocation by 2027.
🟠 CoWoS Packaging — Easing (2025)
Was the #1 bottleneck in late 2024. TSMC doubled capacity by mid-2025. The top 4 AI designers still consume 80–85% of total CoWoS output but headroom has improved. Will retighten as GB300/Rubin ramp.
🟢 EUV Equipment — Structural Scarcity
ASML 12–18 month lead times. High-NA EUV at $400M/unit. No competitor. US/Dutch export controls permanently exclude China from advanced nodes. Scarcity is by design and geopolitical guarantee.

Key Investment Takeaways

✅ Structural Winners
  • NVIDIA: GPU monopoly in AI training. 71% gross margin. $130B+ revenue with pricing power.
  • TSMC: Irreplaceable foundry. Pricing hikes annual. CoWoS monopoly. AI capex beneficiary.
  • Broadcom: Custom ASIC + networking + software (VMware). Best risk-adjusted AI exposure.
  • Micron: HBM3E inflection is secular. AI memory is a 5-year structural tailwind.
⚠️ Watch List
  • ASML: Monopoly is intact but earnings lumpy. High-NA ramp is the multi-year catalyst. Entry points matter.
  • AMD: MI300X AI GPU showing traction but NVIDIA's software moat (CUDA) remains the dominant obstacle.
  • Arm Holdings: Royalty leverage to AI chips is real but valuation pricing in optimistic scenarios.
  • Cadence / Synopsys: Stable compounder; AI-EDA is a new growth vector worth monitoring.
🔴 Structural Challenges
  • Intel: 18A node delays, foundry losses, no AI accelerator. Turnaround is multi-year with no clear catalyst.
  • Samsung: HBM3E yield issues + foundry share loss = margin contraction in both key businesses simultaneously.
  • Amkor / commodity OSAT: Consumer cycle + Asia competition. No differentiation in advanced packaging.
  • Qualcomm: Mobile TAM stagnant; AI PC & auto upside is real but timeline uncertain.
🔑 Core Thesis
  • AI is not a uniform semiconductor boom — it is highly concentrated at 3 nodes: NVIDIA (GPU), TSMC (fab+packaging), SK Hynix (HBM).
  • The bottleneck has migrated: Packaging (2024) → HBM (2025) → Logic nodes (2026+). Track the bottleneck to find the next pricing power company.
  • $800B semiconductor TAM is growing to $1T+ by 2030, but returns will be heavily skewed to monopoly positions and AI-native revenue exposure.
  • Geopolitics (US-China export controls) create permanent structural moats for ASML, TSMC, and US-based memory — an underappreciated floor on pricing power.
