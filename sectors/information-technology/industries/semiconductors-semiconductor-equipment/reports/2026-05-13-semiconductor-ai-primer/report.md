# Global Semiconductor & AI Infrastructure Value Chain: Public Equity Research Primer
*As of May 2026 | Covering: NVIDIA, TSMC, AMD, Intel, Micron, SanDisk, Qualcomm, ASML, Broadcom, Samsung*

***
## Executive Summary
The global semiconductor industry reached approximately $800 billion in revenue in 2025, growing 17.6% year-over-year driven by an insatiable appetite for AI infrastructure. The AI-in-semiconductor sub-market alone was valued at $71.9 billion in 2024 and is projected to reach $321.7 billion by 2033 at an 18.1% CAGR. This primer maps the full value chain — from atom to application — covering business models, margin structures, capex economics, cyclicality, competitive positioning, and valuation frameworks for ten leading public companies. A single semiconductor company surpassed $200 billion in annual revenue for the first time in 2025, illustrating the concentrated but explosive wealth creation unfolding in this sector.[^1][^2][^3][^4]

## Part I: Industry Value Chain Architecture
The semiconductor value chain is one of the most complex and globally interdependent supply chains in existence. It encompasses at least seven distinct functional layers, each with radically different economics, competitive dynamics, and investment profiles.[^5][^6]
### Layer 1 — Electronic Design Automation (EDA) & IP
EDA tools (Synopsys, Cadence, Ansys) and semiconductor IP licensing (ARM) sit at the top of the value chain, providing the software and blueprints that chip designers use to create integrated circuits. This layer enjoys software-like economics: high gross margins (often 70–80%), minimal physical capital intensity, and strong recurring revenue from subscription and royalty models. ARM, whose architecture powers the majority of mobile chips and increasingly server and AI chips, earns royalties on every chip shipped using its ISA — a highly durable and scalable business model.
### Layer 2 — Fabless Chip Design
Fabless companies design chips but outsource all manufacturing to foundries. This model — pioneered by companies like Qualcomm and Broadcom — delivers industry-leading gross margins of 50–75%+ and extremely low capex intensity (typically 1–4% of revenue). NVIDIA, AMD, Qualcomm, and Broadcom all operate this model, enabling them to focus entirely on design innovation and customer acquisition while avoiding the multi-billion-dollar capital expenditures required to build fabs. The fabless market exceeded $270 billion in 2025 and is growing at a 14.4% CAGR to an estimated $530 billion by 2030.[^7][^8][^6]
### Layer 3 — Pure-Play Foundry (Contract Manufacturing)
Foundries manufacture chips designed by fabless customers on a wafer-service fee basis. TSMC dominates this layer with approximately 66% of global foundry revenue and over 90% of leading-edge (3nm and 2nm) manufacturing capacity. The foundry model is deeply capital intensive — TSMC's capex runs at 33–40% of revenue and was guided to $52–56 billion for 2026 — but generates exceptional margins at scale, with TSMC achieving gross margins near 60% and operating margins above 50%. Revenue is transactional per wafer but highly sticky given multi-year customer design-in cycles.[^9][^10][^11]
### Layer 4 — Integrated Device Manufacturers (IDMs)
IDMs design, manufacture, package, and sell their own chips vertically integrated. Intel, Samsung, Micron, and SanDisk operate this model. IDMs historically commanded pricing power through vertical control but face structural headwinds from the superior efficiency of the fabless + foundry horizontal separation. The IDM model requires enormous capital investment, making profitability extremely sensitive to capacity utilization — a major driver of the semiconductor cycle. Intel's capex has run at 28–47% of revenue in recent years during its process-node catch-up program, while Micron's capex averaged 33–49% of revenue to fund memory capacity expansion.[^12]
### Layer 5 — Memory & Storage
Memory (DRAM, NAND, HBM) is the most commoditized yet increasingly strategic segment of the value chain. DRAM is dominated by Samsung, SK Hynix, and Micron; NAND by Samsung, Kioxia (partnered with WD/SanDisk), SK Hynix, Micron, and SanDisk. High-Bandwidth Memory (HBM) — the specialized stacked DRAM required by AI accelerators — has become the hottest growth driver, with Micron projecting HBM's TAM reaching $100 billion by 2028 at a 40% CAGR. SK Hynix leads HBM with an estimated 56% market share in 2025, followed by Samsung at 26% and Micron at 18%. Memory revenues are intensely cyclical, characterized by boom-bust pricing driven by supply-demand imbalances.[^13][^14]
### Layer 6 — Semiconductor Manufacturing Equipment (SME)
Equipment makers supply the physical machinery — lithography systems, etch tools, deposition systems, metrology — that foundries use to manufacture chips. ASML holds a singular position as the sole producer of EUV (Extreme Ultraviolet) lithography systems, which are required to manufacture chips at 7nm and below. Without ASML's EUV tools, TSMC, Samsung, and Intel cannot produce leading-edge chips. Each EUV system sells for roughly €180 million (Low-NA) or €380 million (High-NA), creating a powerful, structurally protected business with a backlog of €38.8 billion at year-end 2025.[^15][^16]
### Layer 7 — Advanced Packaging & OSAT
As transistor scaling becomes increasingly difficult and expensive, advanced packaging (chiplet integration, 3D stacking, CoWoS, HBM attachment) has become a critical differentiator. OSATs (Outsourced Semiconductor Assembly and Test) — ASE, Amkor, JCET — provide packaging and test services. TSMC's CoWoS (Chip-on-Wafer-on-Substrate) packaging capacity has been a major bottleneck in NVIDIA's ability to scale Blackwell shipments, highlighting how packaging has become strategically as important as fabrication itself.[^17]

The structural divergence between the fabless model (low capex, high margins) and the IDM/foundry model (high capex, lower margins) is one of the most important investment insights in the sector. NVIDIA achieves a 56% net margin on $216 billion in revenue while spending only 2.8% of revenue on capex — an almost unparalleled asset-light economics in capital-intensive hardware. In contrast, Micron and Intel invest 27–42% of revenue in capex, with margins that are far more volatile and cycle-dependent.
### The Fabless Margin Premium: Why NVIDIA Earns Like Software
NVIDIA's gross margins of 71% actually exceed those of major SaaS companies — higher than Zoom, CrowdStrike, and Snowflake — defying conventional expectations for a hardware company. Three structural factors underpin this:[^18]

1. **Demand-supply imbalance**: Big Tech ordered millions of Blackwell chips beyond what NVIDIA could build through 2026, enabling premium pricing. The entire 2025 Blackwell production was reportedly sold out by late 2024.[^19]
2. **Ecosystem lock-in**: NVIDIA's CUDA platform has accumulated 25,000 libraries and 4 million developers, making migration to competitors' chips a 6–12 month engineering project.[^18]
3. **Scale economics**: As chip output doubled, packaging costs fell while prices still rose 25% year-over-year.[^18]

## Part III: Competitive Positioning of the Ten Companies
### NVIDIA (NVDA) — The AI Compute Monopolist
NVIDIA controls over 90% of the AI chip market and over 92% of the discrete GPU market. Its H100/H200 and Blackwell GPU architectures are the de facto standard for AI training and inference at hyperscale. Total revenue grew from $26.9 billion in FY2022 to $215.9 billion in FY2026 — an 8x increase in four years driven entirely by AI data center demand. The company's market cap of [$5.52T](pplx://action/claim?id=42) makes it the most valuable publicly listed company in the world as of May 2026. Key risks include customer concentration among four hyperscalers, emerging competition from custom ASICs (Broadcom, Google TPU, Amazon Trainium), and U.S. export controls limiting China market access. NVIDIA's P/E of [46.4x](pplx://action/claim?id=43) implies continued hypergrowth expectations.[^20]
### TSMC (TSM) — The Only Foundry That Can Do It
TSMC manufactured approximately 66% of all global foundry revenue in 2025 and holds a near-monopoly on leading-edge logic at 3nm and below. Virtually every major AI chip — NVIDIA's Blackwell, Apple's A-series, AMD's MI300 — is manufactured by TSMC. The company's FY2025 operating margin reached 50.8%, exceptional for a capital-intensive manufacturer. TSMC is executing a global expansion with announced investments of $165 billion in U.S. fabs, a Kumamoto fab in Japan, and a Dresden fab in Germany — each diluting near-term margins by 2–4 percentage points per year due to overseas operating overhead. Its market cap of [$2.08T](pplx://action/claim?id=44) at a P/E of [34.2x](pplx://action/claim?id=45) reflects both its structural importance and geopolitical premium.[^10][^17][^21]
### AMD (AMD) — The Credible Challenger
AMD is the primary alternative to NVIDIA in AI accelerators and to Intel in server CPUs. Its MI300X GPU and MI300A APU have gained traction at hyperscalers, and AMD reported over 30% year-over-year growth in data center revenue in 2025, with total revenue rising 34% to $34.6 billion. Its gross margin improved to 50% from 46% in FY2023, reflecting a better mix toward premium data center products. AMD's fabless model (capex at only 2.9% of revenue) means it can grow without proportional capital outlays. The current P/E of [149x](pplx://action/claim?id=46) is elevated due to near-breakeven operating margins of 11% even as revenues surge — the gap between gross and operating margin reflects AMD's heavy R&D investment ($8.1 billion, or ~23% of revenue).[^22]
### Intel (INTC) — The IDM Turnaround Story
Intel is executing the most complex corporate turnaround in semiconductor history: simultaneously defending its core x86 CPU business while building a world-class foundry services business. After reporting operating losses of ~$7 billion in its foundry segment in 2023, Intel's 18A process node — featuring RibbonFET GAA transistors and backside power delivery — entered high-volume manufacturing in H2 2025 at Fab 52 in Arizona. Panther Lake processors became the first products built on 18A to ship commercially in early 2026. Microsoft and Amazon have announced foundry agreements on 18A, with signed lifetime commitments exceeding $15 billion. Despite this progress, Intel's gross margin remains at 35% vs. TSMC's 60%, and the company recorded an operating loss of -4% in FY2025 with capex at 28% of revenue. Intel's [$607B](pplx://action/claim?id=47) market cap reflects a deep discount due to execution uncertainty; the stock trades at a negative P/E, with recovery entirely contingent on 18A yield ramp and foundry customer conversion.[^23][^24][^25]
### Micron (MU) — The HBM Beneficiary
Micron is the only U.S.-based DRAM and NAND manufacturer, making it both strategically critical and politically supported (over $200 billion in announced U.S. investment). The company has been transformed by AI-driven HBM demand: from a loss-making FY2023 (gross margin of -9%) to FY2025 gross margin of 40% and net margin of 23%. HBM is fundamentally a different product than commodity DRAM — stacked, custom-configured, with customer-specific logic dies — meaning it cannot be easily substituted, reducing Micron's historical commodity exposure. Micron holds approximately 18% of the HBM market and is ramping HBM4, which incorporates custom logic, increasing switching costs further. At a P/E of [38x](pplx://action/claim?id=48) and capex/revenue at 42%, valuation depends on whether the HBM cycle proves structural or another boom-bust peak.[^21][^14]
### SanDisk (SNDK) — Pure-Play NAND in the AI Era
Spun out of Western Digital in February 2025, SanDisk is now a pure-play NAND developer and manufacturer. Its enterprise SSD business surged, with data center revenues growing 76% year-over-year to $440 million in fiscal Q2. The BiCS8 NAND node (QLC and TLC variants) is being qualified at multiple hyperscalers for Stargate-class AI infrastructure builds. As a recently listed company (traded from February 2025), SNDK's market cap of [$213B](pplx://action/claim?id=49) at a P/E of [49x](pplx://action/claim?id=50) prices in significant enterprise SSD market share gains. Key risk: NAND pricing is historically the most volatile segment of memory, with prices moving 30%+ in single quarters during down-cycles.[^26][^27]
### ASML (ASML) — The Indispensable Monopoly
ASML is the only company in the world producing EUV lithography systems. Without ASML's tools, no chip can be manufactured below 7nm at scale — the resolution where all modern AI chips (NVIDIA Blackwell, Apple A18, AMD MI300) reside. This is a structural monopoly protected by 40+ years of IP, a €9+ billion R&D investment in the EUV light source system, and a supply chain of 5,000+ suppliers including Zeiss (optics) and Cymer (laser systems) that competitors would require decades to replicate. Low-NA EUV machines sell for ~€180M, High-NA for ~€380M each. Year-end 2025 order backlog stood at €38.8 billion, providing multi-year revenue visibility. ASML's installed-base services (30%+ of revenue) provide high-margin recurring cash flows. A potential competitive threat (xLight's free-electron laser, funded with $150M by the U.S. government) targets the light source component but is unlikely to produce commercial silicon before 2028. ASML trades at P/E of [52x](pplx://action/claim?id=51), reflecting its unique monopoly positioning within a cyclical industry.[^15][^16][^28][^29][^30]
### Broadcom (AVGO) — The Custom AI ASIC King
Broadcom is the leading partner for hyperscalers seeking custom AI accelerators (XPUs/ASICs) as alternatives to NVIDIA's off-the-shelf GPUs. The company commands an estimated 60–70% market share in AI ASIC design services and is working with Alphabet (Google TPU), Meta, Apple, ByteDance, and four additional undisclosed hyperscalers. AI semiconductor revenue grew 46% year-over-year to $4.4 billion in Q2 FY2025, with Q4 FY2025 guidance of $6.2 billion. CEO Hock Tan has projected Broadcom's AI serviceable addressable market (SAM) from existing customers alone at $60–90 billion by 2027, before the four new undisclosed customers are included. Broadcom's FY2025 revenue reached $63.9 billion at a 68% gross margin — the second-highest in the peer group after NVIDIA — reflecting the software-like economics of ASIC design. The VMware acquisition ($61 billion in 2023) added an infrastructure software division growing at 25% YoY, diversifying revenue and margin structure. At a P/E of [81x](pplx://action/claim?id=52), the market prices substantial AI ASIC revenue expansion.[^31][^32][^33][^34][^35]
### Qualcomm (QCOM) — The Mobile Giant Pivoting to Edge AI
Qualcomm dominates 5G modem chips and SoCs for premium Android smartphones, capturing ~60%+ of the addressable flagship phone market. It is now executing an aggressive diversification: targeting $22 billion in non-handset revenue by 2029 through automotive, IoT, industrial, and on-device AI. The Snapdragon Digital Chassis platform has established Qualcomm as a top-tier automotive semiconductor player (EBITDA margins ~30% in automotive vs. ~5% for tier-1 suppliers), with platforms entering vehicles in 2026. The AI PC angle — Snapdragon X Elite displacing Intel in premium Windows notebooks — adds an additional growth vector. Revenue grew 14% to $44.3 billion in FY2025 with gross margin at 55%. The core risk is Apple's planned in-house modem transition reducing Qualcomm's addressable market in iPhones, the world's highest-margin device category. P/E of [22.9x](pplx://action/claim?id=53) is the cheapest in the fabless peer group, arguably reflecting excessive near-term pessimism about Apple modem displacement.[^36][^37]
### Samsung (SSUN.F) — The IDM Giant Under Pressure
Samsung Electronics is the world's largest semiconductor company by revenue ($66.5 billion in 2024) encompassing Memory (DRAM, NAND, HBM), Foundry, and Logic/SLSI. Samsung reclaimed the #1 semiconductor vendor position by revenue in 2024 with 10.6% market share, but faces an existential challenge in its foundry business, which has struggled to match TSMC's node yields. Samsung's HBM market share fell from 41% to 17% due to U.S. export restrictions and quality certification delays with NVIDIA. SK Hynix surpassed Samsung in both memory market share and annual operating profit for the first time in 2025. Samsung's "IDM 2.0" strategy — combining memory, foundry, and advanced packaging into turnkey AI solutions — represents an innovative but unproven attempt to differentiate. At a P/E of [28.8x](pplx://action/claim?id=54), Samsung trades at a discount to pure-play peers, arguably offering value if its HBM and foundry turnarounds materialize.[^38][^39][^40][^41][^42]

## Part IV: Cyclicality Framework
### The Semiconductor Cycle: Structure and Triggers
There have been 14 recessive growth periods in the semiconductor industry since 1963 — an average of one every 4.4 years. The cycles are driven by demand-side shocks rather than supply overcapacity (though overcapacity amplifies severity). The causal chain typically begins with: (1) a major technology inflection triggers demand euphoria → (2) customers over-order inventory → (3) fabs commit to long-lead-time capacity additions → (4) demand normalizes or macro deteriorates → (5) inventory glut + new capacity = severe pricing and margin contraction.[^43][^44][^45]
### Current Cycle: AI Supercycle or Extended Upcycle?
As of mid-2026, the industry is in an AI-driven upcycle that has already extended beyond the typical four-year cycle duration. Morningstar (September 2025) warned that "strong AI-related sales and investments are helping the sector to stretch the limits of an upcycle" while noting that semiconductor billings growth has started to slow and AI spending may have peaked in 2025 with slowdown risks in 2026. The two-tier nature of the current cycle is critical:[^46]

- **AI/data center semiconductors** (NVIDIA Blackwell GPUs, HBM, networking chips): structural shortage, pricing power, no near-term cyclical risk from inventory build
- **Consumer/PC/smartphone semiconductors**: weak demand, inventory normalization, conventional cyclicality

This divergence means the headline semiconductor market disguises very different sub-sector dynamics. Memory (particularly NAND) and consumer-exposed foundry nodes are at high cyclical risk; AI compute chips and HBM remain in structural undersupply.

| Business Model | Cyclicality | Reason |
|---------------|-------------|--------|

| Fabless Mobile SoC (Qualcomm) | Moderate | Tied to smartphone replacement cycle; 3-year design-in cycles |

## Part V: Key Demand Drivers
### AI Infrastructure Buildout
AI infrastructure investment is the dominant macro force reshaping semiconductor demand. IDC projects the compute semiconductor segment to grow 36% to $349 billion in 2025 with a 12% five-year CAGR through 2030. AI infrastructure spending is projected to reach $6.7 trillion by 2030. The scale is staggering: NVIDIA's three largest hyperscaler customers (Microsoft, Google, Amazon, Meta) each plan to deploy one million AI chip clusters by 2027.[^1][^47][^33]
### The Memory Supercycle
AI workloads are not just compute-hungry — they are memory-bandwidth-hungry. An NVIDIA H100 GPU requires 80GB of HBM3 memory; a Blackwell B200 uses 192GB of HBM3E. As models scale, the "storage crisis" has become as limiting as compute bottlenecks. SK Hynix expects the AI memory market to grow 30% per year to 2030. HBM specifically is projected to grow from $2.9 billion in 2024 to $16.7 billion by 2033 at a 21% CAGR.[^48][^49][^50]
### Sovereign AI and Geopolitical Reshoring
U.S., EU, Japanese, and Korean governments are investing aggressively in domestic semiconductor capacity. In the U.S. alone, over $480 billion in semiconductor-related investments were announced in 2024–2025, including $165 billion from TSMC, $200 billion from Micron, $60 billion from Texas Instruments, and $40 billion from Samsung. The CHIPS and Science Act (2022) provided $52 billion in direct subsidies. China's export restrictions on rare earth elements (expanded in October 2025) have added a new dimension of supply chain vulnerability. These geopolitical dynamics structurally increase long-term semiconductor capex, benefiting equipment makers and foundries.[^21][^51]
### Edge AI and the Endpoint Explosion
As AI models become smaller (distillation, quantization) and more efficient, AI computation is migrating from data centers to edge devices — smartphones, PCs, automotive, IoT. Qualcomm's Snapdragon X Elite is the leading edge-AI PC platform; its Snapdragon Cockpit Elite and Ride Elite platforms target automotive AI cockpit and ADAS. This second wave of AI semiconductor demand will benefit mobile SoC designers (Qualcomm, MediaTek) and on-device memory vendors.[^36][^52]

## Part VI: Key Investment Risks
### 1. AI CapEx Concentration and Demand Cliff
Approximately 70–80% of AI chip revenue is concentrated in four hyperscalers. If cloud providers' AI ROI does not materialize as expected — revenue from AI services failing to justify compute investments — capex could retrench sharply. Morningstar flags this as the primary bear case for the entire AI semiconductor complex.[^46]
### 2. Geopolitical Fragmentation and Export Controls
U.S. export controls prohibit shipment of A100/H100-class chips and ASML's EUV tools to China. China represented ~20–25% of semiconductor equipment sales in 2024 and is building domestic alternatives (SMIC, Huawei). Escalating U.S.-China tech decoupling risks stranded assets and market share losses for U.S. and European companies. ASML saw China fall from 36% to 19% of system sales in Q1 2026.[^16][^28]
### 3. Manufacturing Technology Execution Risk
Intel's entire thesis depends on 18A node ramp with acceptable yields (>60% threshold). Samsung's foundry business must close the yield gap with TSMC on 3nm and below. Any node delay or yield disappointment has been historically devastating to semiconductor stock valuations.[^23]
### 4. Custom ASIC Cannibalization of NVIDIA
Every dollar spent on Broadcom-designed custom ASICs (Google TPU, Meta MTIA, Amazon Trainium) is potentially a dollar not spent on NVIDIA GPUs. As the hyperscaler ASIC ecosystem matures — and with four new unnamed customers joining Broadcom's program — NVIDIA's addressable market could narrow in training and inference workloads.[^32]
### 5. Memory Cycle Reversal
NAND pricing showed signs of recovery in late 2025 (SanDisk announced a 10% price hike followed by Micron halting DRAM quotes), but NAND pricing historically reverses sharply when capacity additions meet weaker-than-expected consumer demand. Consumer electronics remain weak, and the AI data center NAND build is unlikely to indefinitely offset this.[^46][^27]
### 6. TSMC Geopolitical Concentration
Taiwan manufactures ~90% of advanced (sub-10nm) logic chips. Any military confrontation between China and Taiwan would be catastrophic for global semiconductor supply — a tail risk that justifies a permanent structural premium discount on TSMC vs. onshore manufacturers.

### Framework 2: EV/Sales and Price/Gross Profit for High-Growth or Loss-Making Companies
For companies like Intel (loss-making), AMD (margin-expansion story), and early-stage hyperscalers, EV/Sales or P/Gross Profit better capture the economics. NVIDIA's EV/Sales of ~21x and AMD's comparatively lower multiple reflect the market's differentiated pricing of NVIDIA's pricing power vs. AMD's commoditized-but-scaling trajectory.
### Framework 3: DCF with Normalization for Cyclical Companies
Memory companies (Micron, SanDisk, Samsung DS) and IDMs (Intel) require mid-cycle or through-cycle normalization rather than current-earnings-based multiples. During memory down-cycles, P/E and EV/EBITDA metrics become meaningless (negative or infinite). A DCF using long-term normalized free cash flow, discounted at a WACC of 9–11% reflecting semiconductor beta, captures the true through-cycle value.[^54][^55]
### Framework 4: Replacement Value and Strategic Option Value for Foundries
TSMC and Intel Foundry are partially valued on replacement value of their fabrication capacity — it would cost an estimated $10–20 billion per leading-edge fab to replicate. Government subsidies and strategic importance create option value that a pure earnings-based model understates. ASML's monopoly moat commands a structural premium of ~30–50% over traditional semiconductor equipment EV/EBITDA multiples.[^6]
### Framework 5: Sum-of-the-Parts (SOTP) for Conglomerates
Samsung, Broadcom, and Qualcomm are best analyzed via SOTP given their multi-segment structures. Broadcom's semiconductor ASIC division (growing 46% YoY) warrants a 20–30x forward earnings multiple, while its VMware infrastructure software division deserves a SaaS-like 15–20x EBITDA multiple. The blended multiple of [81x](pplx://action/claim?id=52) trailing P/E reflects the software mix enriching the combined entity.[^31]
### Key Valuation Sensitivities by Company
- **NVIDIA**: Hinge on long-term AI capex trajectory and custom ASIC cannibalization pace. A $350–$400 CAGR revenue scenario implies 40%+ ROIC sustainability. Bear case if AI capex decelerates; bull case if sovereign AI and robotics add a third major demand driver.
- **TSMC**: Overseas fab dilution (2–4 ppt/yr through 2029) sets a gross margin ceiling at 53–57%, but AI-driven advanced node pricing power can expand operating leverage. Geopolitical discount is the X-factor.[^17]
- **AMD**: Optionality on MI400/MI500 generation AI GPU share gain and EPYC server CPU continued share wins. Revenue growth rate, not current EPS, is the primary valuation driver.
- **Intel**: Binary event risk on 18A yield and foundry ramp. Success scenario (e.g., $20B+ external foundry revenue by 2027–28) could justify 15–20x EBITDA; failure re-rates toward asset liquidation value.
- **Micron**: HBM "structurality" vs. cyclicality is the key debate. Bull case: HBM TAM at $100B by 2028 with Micron at 20%+ share = $20B+ incremental revenue at 50%+ gross margins. Bear case: oversupply in commodity DRAM/NAND triggers 2023-style margin collapse.[^13]
- **ASML**: Monopoly moat supports 40–55x forward P/E throughout the cycle. Export control risk (China ~20% of 2024 revenue) is the primary downside catalyst. High-NA EUV ramp (€380M/unit) from Intel, TSMC, Samsung is the next decade's structural growth driver.[^16][^28]

## Part IX: Industry Structure Trends
### Trend 1: The Horizontal Disaggregation Deepens
The historical IDM model (one company designs, builds, and sells chips) is being replaced by a horizontal structure where design (fabless), manufacturing (foundry), packaging (OSAT/CoWoS), and IP (EDA/ARM) are separated. This creates more specialized, efficient, and investable companies — but also greater coordination risk and geopolitical vulnerability.[^6][^12]
### Trend 2: Chiplets and 3D Integration Replace Monolithic Scaling
Moore's Law scaling (transistor density doubling every 2 years) has slowed dramatically. The industry response is chiplets — combining multiple smaller dies in a single package using advanced interconnects (UCIe, HBI). AMD's Ryzen 7000 series and EPYC Genoa/Turin all use chiplet architectures; NVIDIA's Blackwell uses two GPU dies connected via NVLink Chip2Chip. This extends performance scaling but concentrates value in advanced packaging (CoWoS, SoIC) and interconnect IP.
### Trend 3: Software-Defined Silicon and AI-Driven Design
AI is now accelerating chip design itself — AI-assisted EDA (Google, Synopsys DeepSilicon) can reduce design cycles by 30–40%. NVIDIA's CUDA platform is evolving from a parallel-compute API into a full-stack AI factory operating system. The line between hardware and software in semiconductors is blurring, raising the strategic importance of software, developer ecosystems, and platform lock-in.
### Trend 4: Custom Silicon Proliferation
Every major hyperscaler now has or is building custom silicon: Google TPU (Broadcom partner), Amazon Trainium (custom, Intel 18A roadmap), Microsoft Azure Maia (NVIDIA fallback + custom), Meta MTIA (Broadcom). This trend diversifies AI chip supply but requires enormous upfront R&D and multi-year lead times, benefiting ASIC design partners (Broadcom, Marvell) and foundries (TSMC, Intel 18A).
