## Section 2: Company Overview

### Business Description

Micron Technology is one of only three companies globally (alongside Samsung and SK Hynix) capable of manufacturing leading-edge DRAM and NAND memory at commercial scale. Founded in 1978 in Boise, Idaho, Micron designs, develops, manufactures, and sells memory and storage products across four major end markets: data centers, client (PCs/laptops), mobile, and automotive/embedded.[^8]

The company's products fall into two main categories: **DRAM** (Dynamic Random Access Memory) — which accounted for approximately 79% of FY2025 revenue — and **NAND flash** (~20%). Within DRAM, the fastest-growing sub-segment is **High Bandwidth Memory (HBM)**, a premium DRAM variant critical for AI accelerators such as NVIDIA's Blackwell GPUs. HBM is manufactured by vertically stacking 8 or 12 DRAM dies connected with through-silicon vias (TSVs), commanding prices approximately four times that of standard DRAM.[^9]

### Business Units

Micron operates through four reporting segments:
- **Cloud Memory Business Unit (CMBU):** AI/HBM, server DRAM, data center SSDs. Q2 FY2026 revenue of $7.7B (32% of total, 74% gross margin).[^2]
- **Core Data Center Business Unit (CDBU):** Enterprise server memory. Q2 FY2026 revenue $5.7B (24% of total).[^2]
- **Mobile & Client Business Unit:** Consumer DRAM/NAND for smartphones and PCs.
- **Automotive & Embedded Business Unit:** DRAM/NAND for automotive, industrial, IoT.

### Management Team

**Sanjay Mehrotra** serves as Chairman, President, and CEO since May 2017. His near-decade tenure has coincided with Micron's transformation from a pure commodity memory maker into a technology-differentiated player. Total FY2025 compensation was ~$30.9M (4.7% salary, 95.3% equity-linked), strongly aligning management with shareholders. **Mark Murphy** serves as Executive VP & CFO with ~4 years of tenure. The management team's average tenure is 5.4 years.[^10]

### Capital Allocation

Micron has been disciplined in capital allocation during its upcycle. In Q2 FY2026, the company generated record free cash flow of $6.9B (77% above the prior quarterly record), repurchased $350M of shares, and **reduced debt by $1.6 billion** including redemption of 2029 senior notes — closing Q2 with the highest net cash position in company history. The FY2026 capex plan of ~$20B (up from $18B prior) is focused on HBM capacity and 1-gamma DRAM supply, with global facilities across Idaho, New York, Japan, Singapore, and India.[^5][^4][^2]

## Section 3: Industry & Competitive Analysis

### Memory Industry Dynamics

The global DRAM market is a structural oligopoly with only three relevant players: SK Hynix, Samsung, and Micron. As of Q1 2025, SK Hynix held 36% DRAM market share (the first time it surpassed Samsung), Samsung held 34%, and Micron held 25%. By Q3 2025, Micron's DRAM market share climbed to 25.7%, up 3.7 percentage points quarter-over-quarter, as it accelerated HBM3E shipments and gained share from Samsung's HBM qualification struggles.[^11][^12][^7]

The structural demand driver is **HBM for AI data centers**. HBM revenue is expected to grow from approximately $35 billion in 2025 to $100 billion in 2028, a ~40% CAGR as confirmed by Micron management. The HBM "trade ratio" — where each HBM wafer consumes ~3x the DRAM wafer equivalent of standard DDR5 — severely tightens overall DRAM supply, creating a feedback loop that benefits all three incumbents.[^5][^4]

**Supply discipline is unusually strong.** Micron's entire CY2026 HBM supply is committed under multi-year price and volume agreements. Management expects tight industry conditions to persist through and beyond CY2026, with both DRAM and NAND bit shipments constrained at ~20% growth against similar demand growth.[^4][^5]

Micron's HBM competitive advantages include: (1) a simpler, more energy-efficient design versus SK Hynix's, (2) early 1-gamma node transition providing better cost/density, and (3) direct NVIDIA preference. KeyBanc noted that "NVDA is increasingly prioritizing the use of MU's HBM memory on Blackwell" due to minor performance issues at SK Hynix, though MU's total addressable volume is capped by capacity.[^7]

## Section 4: Financial Analysis

### Revenue Trends

Micron's revenue profile reflects the inherent volatility of the memory cycle, but with an AI-driven secular inflection layer now superimposed. Revenue contracted -49.5% in FY2023 (to $15.5B) due to a severe DRAM/NAND price downturn, before recovering +48.9% to $37.4B in FY2025. The acceleration to FY2026E consensus revenue of $109.3B represents +192% growth — an unprecedented step-change driven primarily by HBM pricing and volume.[^13][^1][^3]

The data center segment reached 56% of company revenue in FY2025, with HBM alone approaching $2 billion in Q4 FY2025. By Q2 FY2026, CMBU (Cloud Memory) was the largest segment at $7.7B with 74% gross margins — vs. the company's blended 75% consolidated gross margin — demonstrating that the premium mix shift is carrying through to all product lines.[^14][^2]

### Margin Expansion

The margin trajectory at Micron is exceptional by any historical standard. Gross margins collapsed to -9.1% in FY2023, recovered to 22.4% in FY2024, and exited FY2025 at 39.8% adjusted gross margin. The Q2 FY2026 consolidated gross margin of 75% (GAAP), guided to expand further in Q3 FY2026, reflects both pricing power from sold-out HBM supply and favorable mix toward premium HBM vs. commodity DRAM/NAND.[^15][^13][^2]

EBITDA margins are tracking toward ~81% in FY2026E — an astonishing figure for a hardware manufacturer and reflective of the structural pricing power HBM now commands at $1.49/unit vs. $0.07 for NAND.[^9]

### Balance Sheet & Leverage

Micron's balance sheet is conservative for a capital-intensive semiconductor manufacturer. As of FY2025A, total assets were $82.8B, total equity $54.2B, total debt $14.6B (LT debt $14.0B), and cash $9.6B, implying net debt of approximately $5.6B. The debt-to-equity ratio has declined to ~0.15x — one of the lowest in the sector. Post Q2 FY2026 debt paydown of $1.6B, Micron moved into a **net cash position** for the first time in years.[^16][^17][^18][^2]

Working capital trends reflect the inventory cycle: days inventory outstanding (DIO) peaked at ~162 days in the FY2023 trough and normalized to ~140 days by FY2025. CapEx intensity (CapEx/revenue) has declined from 49% (FY2023) to 37% (FY2025), and should continue contracting toward ~18% of projected FY2026 revenue despite the absolute dollar increase.[^13]

### Sector-Specific Risk: Memory Cyclicality

Memory semiconductors are among the most cyclical in the technology sector, as evidenced by the 49% revenue decline in FY2023. The key differentiator in this cycle is the **structural nature of HBM demand** — not driven by consumer inventory cycles, but by hyperscaler capex commitments tied to AI infrastructure buildouts with 3-5 year planning horizons. Micron management described supply as "substantially short of demand for the foreseeable future" and confirmed that multi-year customer agreements now cover full calendar 2026 and into 2027 HBM allocation. This shifts the revenue visibility from quarterly to annual-plus, partially de-risking the traditional memory downside scenario.[^13][^5]
