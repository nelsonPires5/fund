## Section 2: Company Overview

### Business Description

Founded in 1987 by Morris Chang in Hsinchu, Taiwan, TSMC pioneered the pure-play foundry model — manufacturing chips designed by other companies without competing in chip design itself. This model proved transformative: it allowed fabless companies (NVIDIA, AMD, Qualcomm, Apple, Broadcom) to focus entirely on design innovation while TSMC concentrated all capital and engineering resources on manufacturing excellence. The company went public in 1997 and today employs approximately 65,000 people globally.

TSMC's revenue model is wafer-service fee based: customers pay a per-wafer price that varies by process node, volume commitment, and customization requirements. This is transactional rather than recurring in the subscription sense, but is **highly sticky** given multi-year customer design-in cycles (typically 2–4 years from design to tape-out) that create enormous switching costs. Advanced-node wafers at N2 are priced at approximately $30,000–$33,000 per 300mm wafer, up 10% from the prior generation, with 4nm experiencing 10–30% price increases in 2026.[^7][^8]

### Revenue Breakdown

Platform mix has shifted decisively toward AI over the past five years:

| Platform | FY2021 | FY2022 | FY2023 | FY2024 | FY2025 |
|----------|--------|--------|--------|--------|--------|
| HPC / AI | 37% | 41% | 43% | 51% | 58% |
| Smartphones | 44% | 39% | 38% | 35% | 29% |
| IoT | 8% | 9% | 8% | 6% | 5% |
| Automotive | 4% | 5% | 6% | 5% | 5% |
| DCE + Other | 7% | 6% | 5% | 3% | 3% |

Geographically, North America (predominantly Apple, NVIDIA, AMD, Broadcom, Qualcomm) generated **75% of FY2025 revenue**, up from 65% in 2021. China exposure fell from 12% in 2023 to 9% in 2025 as US export controls constrained advanced-node shipments to Chinese customers.

By process node, advanced nodes (7nm and below) represented approximately 74% of wafer revenue in Q3 2025, with 3nm at 25% and 5nm at 36% of wafer revenue in Q1 2026.[^4]

### Management Team

CEO C.C. Wei has led TSMC since 2018 and has executed one of the most ambitious global expansions in technology history. Under Wei's leadership, TSMC has committed $165 billion to US fabs, expanded to Japan and Germany, and maintained technology leadership through N3, N2, and now A14 (1.4nm) development. CFO Jen-Chua Huang provided Q2 2026 guidance on the Q1 2026 earnings call. The management team has an exceptional track record of guidance accuracy — Q1 2026 revenue of $35.9 billion beat the $34.6–$35.8 billion guidance, with gross margin at 66.2% exceeding the guided 63–65% range.[^9][^6][^10][^4]

### Recent Strategic Developments

- **Q2 2026 guidance**: Revenue $39.0–$40.2 billion, gross margin 65.5–67.5%.[^10]

- **N2 node**: HVM since Q4 2025 with favorable yields; ramping to 100,000 WPM by end-2026.[^3]

- **CapEx 2026**: Approaching upper end of $52–$56 billion guidance.[^11]

## Section 3: Industry and Competitive Analysis

### Industry Overview and TAM

The global semiconductor industry reached approximately $800 billion in revenue in 2025, growing 17.6% YoY driven by AI infrastructure demand. The pure-play foundry sub-sector is a critical layer in the value chain: every fabless chip designer — from NVIDIA to Apple — depends on foundries to translate designs into silicon. The foundry TAM was approximately $120–130 billion in 2025, with a projected long-term CAGR of 12–15%.

AI is the dominant demand driver reshaping the semiconductor landscape. IDC projects the compute semiconductor segment to grow 36% to $349 billion in 2025 with a 12% five-year CAGR through 2030. TSMC's management noted the transition from "generative AI" to "agentic AI" is driving increased token consumption and sustained demand for advanced-node chips. AI accelerator demand has a projected CAGR in the mid-to-high 50s% through 2029.[^2]

### Competitive Landscape

TSMC's competitive position is qualitatively different from any peer:

| Company | Role | Advanced Node Capability | Market Share | Key Strength |
|---------|------|--------------------------|-------------|--------------|
| **TSMC** | Pure-play foundry | N2, N3, N5 HVM | ~66% foundry revenue; >90% ≤3nm | Technology leadership, customer trust |
| Samsung | IDM + Foundry | 3nm GAA (yield issues) | ~10-12% foundry | Memory + foundry integration |
| Intel Foundry | IDM + Foundry | 18A (entering HVM H2 2026) | <5% advanced | US onshore; 18A RibbonFET |
| GlobalFoundries | Pure-play | Mature nodes (22nm+) | ~7% foundry | Specialty/defense, US-based |
| UMC | Pure-play | 28nm and above | ~5% foundry | Mature nodes, lower cost |

Samsung's foundry business has struggled to match TSMC's node yields, and Samsung's HBM market share fell from 41% to 17% partly due to quality issues. Intel's 18A is entering HVM in H2 2026 with signed commitments exceeding $15 billion (Microsoft, Amazon), but gross margin remains at 35% vs. TSMC's 60%, and Intel's foundry segment recorded operating losses.

### Competitive Advantages (Moats)

TSMC's moat is rated **High durability** by the research primer, resting on three pillars:

1. **Process leadership and IP accumulation**: Decades of process refinement have created a manufacturing knowledge base — equipment configurations, process recipes, yield management techniques — that cannot be replicated quickly. TSMC's N5-to-N3 yield learning curve took 3+ years and billions in investment; Intel is still catching up after a decade-long effort.
2. **Customer trust and design-in cycles**: Every chip designed for TSMC's process node uses its PDKs (Process Design Kits). Migrating to another foundry requires redesigning the entire chip — a 2–4 year, $500M+ commitment. Apple has never taped out a production chip at any other advanced foundry.
3. **Scale and ecosystem**: TSMC's $52–56 billion CapEx in 2026 alone dwarfs what any competitor can commit. This creates self-reinforcing advantages: more revenue → more CapEx → more advanced nodes → more customers → more revenue.[^11]

The primary competitive threat is Intel 18A, which uses backside power delivery and RibbonFET transistors — technically equal to or slightly ahead of TSMC N2 in some metrics — but Intel's yield maturity, customer ecosystem, and foundry track record lag significantly.

## Section 4: Financial Analysis

### Revenue and Margin Trends

TSMC's historical revenue trajectory reflects its technology cycle, with the 2023 trough caused by inventory digestion following the 2022 PC/smartphone excess, followed by an AI-driven acceleration:

- **FY2021**: $56.9B | **FY2022**: $74.2B (+30.4%) | **FY2023**: $68.6B (–7.6%) | **FY2024**: $89.3B (+30.2%) | **FY2025**: $120.6B (+35.9%)

The gross margin trajectory is equally compelling: TSMC's gross margin expanded from 51.6% in FY2021 to 59.9% in FY2025, driven by three structural factors: (1) advanced-node mix shift (higher-ASP N3/N2 as % of wafer revenue), (2) pricing power exercised through 3–10% annual price increases on advanced nodes, and (3) operating leverage on fixed R&D and overhead costs as revenue scales.[^14][^8]

Q1 2026 gross margin reached **66.2%**, significantly above the guided 63–65% range, demonstrating that pricing power and yield improvements are outpacing overseas fab cost headwinds.[^9][^4]

### Capital Intensity and CapEx Economics

TSMC's foundry model is deeply capital-intensive — CapEx runs at 33–40% of revenue — but generates exceptional returns at scale. The capital intensity is structural: each new leading-edge fab requires $15–20 billion and 4–5 years to build, creating massive barriers to entry. TSMC has guided $52–56 billion in CapEx for 2026, the highest in its history.[^11]

The CapEx/Depreciation ratio has been running at approximately 1.4–1.8x, meaning TSMC consistently invests more than it depreciates, reflecting a growth company building capacity ahead of demand. In 2025, this ratio rose to approximately 1.85x, reflecting the Arizona investment ramp.

### Balance Sheet and Liquidity

TSMC maintains a net cash position of approximately **$64.1 billion** at end-2025 despite massive capital outlays. Total debt is approximately $1.065 trillion NTD, more than offset by $2.064 trillion NTD in cash and investments at a 32.2 NTD/USD exchange rate. The current ratio was approximately 2.5x in FY2025, and the company has no near-term debt maturity concerns.

### Return Metrics

TSMC's capital efficiency has improved dramatically:

ROIC of 43.8% in FY2025 vs. a WACC of approximately 10.2% implies an economic value spread of 33.6 percentage points — one of the widest in the technology sector.

## Section 5: Projections and Assumptions

### Revenue Projection Methodology

Revenue is projected from two inputs: (1) end-market demand growth drivers by platform (HPC/AI, smartphone, automotive, IoT) and (2) TSMC's pricing power and capacity expansion trajectory.

**HPC/AI** (projected to reach ~63% of mix by FY2026): AI accelerator demand CAGR is guided by management in the mid-to-high 50s% through 2029. The secular drivers are: agentic AI adoption driving increased token consumption, continued hyperscaler CapEx ($52B+ planned for each of the top 4 hyperscalers through 2027), and TSMC's near-monopoly on sub-3nm production.[^2]

**Smartphones** (declining from 29% to ~17% by FY2030): Premium smartphones still require advanced nodes (Apple A19 on N2), but the volume growth is modest. Smartphone unit volumes are essentially flat globally; revenue growth comes from rising silicon content per phone.

**Automotive** (stable at ~5%): Long-term secular growth in vehicle silicon content (ADAS, EV battery management, infotainment) provides a reliable mid-single-digit growth vector.

## Section 7: Risk Analysis

### R1 — Taiwan Strait Escalation (Severity: CRITICAL | Probability: Low, 5–10%)

Taiwan manufactures over 90% of the world's advanced sub-10nm logic chips. Any military confrontation between China and Taiwan — even a partial blockade — would represent the most catastrophic supply chain disruption in history, destroying trillions in semiconductor supply and the foundational infrastructure for the global AI buildout. Research using tabletop exercises identified that Taiwan's supply chain would be particularly vulnerable to a Chinese quarantine before 2027, as overseas fabs are not yet ramped to compensate. TSMC's $165B Arizona commitment is the primary long-term mitigant, but near-term, the risk is largely unhedgeable.[^16][^17][^18][^5]

**Tripwire**: Cross-Strait tension escalation indicators (PLAAF incursion frequency, naval exercises); US-Taiwan defense agreement weakening signals.

### R2 — AI CapEx Deceleration (Severity: HIGH | Probability: Medium, 20–30%)

Approximately 70–80% of AI chip revenue is concentrated in four hyperscalers (Microsoft, Google, Amazon, Meta). If these companies' AI ROI does not materialize (AI services revenue failing to justify compute investment), capex could retrench sharply. Morningstar has flagged this as the primary bear case for the AI semiconductor complex, noting semiconductor billings growth has started to slow and AI spending may have peaked in 2025.

**Mitigant**: TSMC's diversified customer base (Apple, Qualcomm, AMD also drive demand); automotive secular growth; Q1 2026 results showing no sign of demand moderation.

### R3 — N2 Yield / Gross Margin Disappointment (Severity: HIGH | Probability: Medium, 15–20%)

N2 wafers cost approximately $30,000–$33,000 each; early ramp yields below 80–85% could delay the inflection where N2 gross margins exceed the corporate average. TSMC guided that N2/overseas fab cost headwinds would dilute gross margins by 2–3% in 2026.[^7][^15][^2]

**Tripwire**: Two consecutive quarters of gross margin below 63%; N2 as % of wafer revenue below 3% by Q3 2026.

### R4 — US-China Trade Escalation (Severity: MEDIUM | Probability: Medium-High, 25–35%)

China has been progressively reduced as a revenue contributor (12% in 2023 → 9% in 2025) through export controls. Further escalation — additional restrictions on advanced packaging, tariffs on Taiwan-made chips — could accelerate this compression.

**Mitigant**: China mix already low; continued shift to North American AI customers offsets.

### R5 — Overseas Fab Structural Cost Dilution (Severity: MEDIUM-LOW | Probability: Very High, >80%)

Arizona, Japan, and Germany fabs carry structurally higher labor, energy, and overhead costs than Taiwan fabs. Management has acknowledged this contributes approximately 2–3% per year of gross margin headwind, expected to persist through 2029. This is essentially a **known** and **manageable** risk — the question is whether N2 pricing power grows fast enough to offset it.[^15][^2]
